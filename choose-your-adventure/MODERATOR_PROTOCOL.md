# Onsite moderator guide — focus study (choose-your-adventure)

Use this **during live sessions** with participants. Pair it with your consent script and incentive plan. It does **not** replace technical setup docs for your team.

---

## What you’re facilitating

Participants sit at a kiosk, **choose one research topic** that fits them, work through **on-screen questions** (and sometimes **clickable prototypes**), then see a **thank-you** screen. Your job is to **welcome them**, **set expectations**, **stay available**, **coach interaction on prototypes** when needed, and **close the session** warmly—without taking over their answers.

---

## One session, end to end

1. **Greet & consent** — Handle consent, NDA, and incentives per your runbook before they start tapping.
2. **Frame the task** — In one or two sentences: they’ll pick a study that sounds like them, answer honestly, and may see a short interactive preview. Give a rough time (e.g. 10–20 minutes). They do the reading and tapping themselves unless they need an accessibility accommodation.
3. **Choose a study** — They land on a screen with **topic cards** (User preferences, My Red Hat, Product evaluation, etc.). They can:
   - **Tap a card** and then **Take the study** (most common).
   - **Random study** — the app picks a topic and opens it automatically after a short animation.
   - **Help me choose** — three short questions, then back to the cards with one highlighted; they still tap **Take the study**.
   - **Choose by role** — they pick a role (e.g. Developer, IT Ops); the app opens the matching study automatically.
4. **Work through the study** — They move with **Next** and **Previous**; the last step is **Submit**. The header shows which study they’re in and page progress.
5. **Thank-you & next steps** — Screen thanks them, mentions swag and an optional community QR. Point them to **swag** and anything else your event requires.
6. **Reset for the next person** — When the station is free, they (or you) tap **Start over** so the next participant starts clean.

---

## When to step in (and when to step back)

| Situation | You |
|-----------|-----|
| **Choosing a topic** | **Step back** while they read—unless your study requires a **specific** topic; then direct them clearly to that card and **Take the study**. If they’re stuck between Random, Help me choose, and cards, give a **one-line** difference (random = surprise; help me choose = three questions first). |
| **Reading and answering** | **Stay quiet** unless they look lost. The screen instructions carry the task. |
| **Prototype / interactive preview** | **Engage.** Ask them to click around like they would in real life, then go forward when they’re ready. Use your **think-aloud** or task script here if you have one. |
| **They can’t advance** | **Next** only works when the page is complete (e.g. every row placed, every ranking filled, enough items selected). If they chose **Other**, they may need to type a short explanation. Help them spot what’s missing—don’t answer for them. |
| **Blank or slow preview** | Ask them to wait a moment, scroll inside the preview area, or check Wi‑Fi with your team if it never loads. |
| **Wrong topic** | Before **Submit**: **Back to study selection** at the top. After **Submit**: **Start over** on the thank-you screen and begin again. |
| **Thank-you** | Thank them, repeat **swag** / **QR** as your runbook says, then reset when they walk away. |

## What each study covers (participant journey)

Use this so you know **what’s coming** and **where prototypes matter**. You don’t need to read these aloud.

### User preferences
How account and preference data should work: **where** they expect to manage different kinds of information, what should follow them **everywhere vs per app**, **which five attributes** matter most to carry across experiences, and how willing they are to share (and how it feels to re-enter information). Drag-and-drop and selection tasks—help if they can’t get **Next** to activate.

### Product evaluation
How they **evaluate** software: experience level, **trust** (e.g. own environment vs quick browser try-out), “modernizer” vs “veteran” style, **blank slate vs AI-prepared template**, then a **budget exercise** (spending “credits” on evaluation options they care about). Follow-up questions react to what they chose. Ends with open reflection. **No** separate prototype block in this track—your coaching is about **understanding** the tradeoff questions.

### My Red Hat
**Three parts in order**, each with an **intro**, then a **clickable preview** (when available), then **written follow-ups**:

1. **Portable My Red Hat** — Tools that follow them across sites; sidekick idea; notifications. **Coach the preview** like a real task.
2. **Generative / intelligent dashboard** — Prompting a blank space, layout vs automation, trust in AI-built views. **Coach the preview**; follow-ups repeat similar themes in the third section.
3. **Proof of value / renewal** — Showing value to leadership; same preview + follow-up pattern as section 2 in spirit (prompt, layout, trust).

If a preview area looks like a **gray placeholder**, your team may still be wiring the design—stay calm, ask them to read the question and answer honestly, and flag it to staff.

### Product marketing
Short **context** on what “a product in a portfolio” means, then **label preference** for how products are grouped, a **sorting task** (where they’d expect different types of content in a menu), then **rank nine menu items** (1 = most through 9 = least important), then **which one menu item they would remove** (nine choices) plus **why**, then **Yes/No** on **consistent** labels across products’ secondary nav plus **required** reasoning, then **Yes/No** on consolidating **Products & documentation** (Red Hat AI–style) across all product secondary nav plus **required** reasoning, then **one open** prompt to **add** a menu item: what it would be, **why**, and what **content types** belong under it.

### Developer program
What **developer tools** they use (multi-choice), then **two open questions** on how resources help them and what would make the program more valuable.

### My trials
How they **start trials**, then **two open questions** on challenges and improvements.

### Content discovery
How they **learn**: intro, **pick a topic**, **numbered resource** choice + **why**, **optional** other resources (image placeholder), then a **branching 4-factor ranking** (video/podcast/event vs blog vs ebook/use case/whitepaper lists), **rank six content types** for the topic, then **Yes/No** on wanting **more** content types; **if Yes**, **open text** for specifics.

## Scripted lines (adapt freely)

**Opening:** “You’ll pick **one** topic that fits you, then go through questions at your own pace. Some topics include a **short interactive preview**—click around like you would at work. About **[10–20] minutes**; there are no wrong answers.”

**Prototype:** “Treat this like a real screen—**tap through** what you’d actually do. When you’re ready, hit **Next**.”

**Think-aloud (optional):** “If you’re comfortable, say what you’re **thinking** as you go.”

**Stuck:** “If **Next** won’t light up, something on this page still needs a tap or a short answer—often a drag-and-drop row or an **Other** box.”

**Closing:** “Thank you—that’s everything here. **Swag** is at **[wherever]**; the **QR** on screen is optional for community. Tap **Start over** when you’re done so we’re ready for the next person.”

---

## Privacy & respect

- Treat everything they type as **sensitive** until your team says otherwise.
- On **shared** kiosks, don’t promise that visuals on screen are “only them”—some summaries aggregate others’ input. Keep expectations honest.

## Staff note (not for participants)

Recording and export are handled per your **field ops / research ops** process. Moderators focus on the **participant experience** above.
